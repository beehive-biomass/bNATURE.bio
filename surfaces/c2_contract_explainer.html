What this contract says

Six things in this agreement that are easy to read past. Every quotation below is verbatim from the document as filed; … marks text omitted for length, never text changed. This page explains what the words do — it does not tell anyone what to do about them.

Before anything else: this document has holes. The filed version is redacted in 21 places, and those include every per-pound price — month one, month two, month three, and the price for the remaining volume. This page cannot tell you what any of this pays, because the filing does not say. Where a redaction sits next to a term explained below, it is marked.
1 · the one most people read past

The commitment is 285,000 lbs. The obligation is 45,000.

"Supplier will sell and Buyer will purchase 285,000 (two hundred eighty five thousand) lbs of Consumable Hemp Flower"Purchase Commitment

That reads as a firm two-sided commitment, and for the first tranche it is. The schedule names three months:

month one — June 201910,000 lb
month two — July 201915,000 lb
month three — August 201920,000 lb
firm subtotal45,000 lb

The remaining 240,000 lb is handled three sections later, in a sentence about pricing:

"Buyer may place monthly Purchase Orders in excess of the Initial Order."Purchase Commitment, pricing paragraph

"May", not "shall." The first sentence says the buyer will purchase 285,000 lb. This one makes 240,000 of it — 84% — something the buyer may order or may not. The obligation does not contradict itself; it changes into an option, in a paragraph about price.

The arithmetic is exactly right — 45,000 + 240,000 = 285,000 — which is part of why this is easy to read past. There is no error to notice. The three sentences are simply never read together.

The standard this is measured byContract language distinguishes shall / will (obligation) from may (option). Where both appear about the same quantity, the operative one is the specific provision governing that quantity — here, the one that says "may."
2 · who decides whether it passed

Two rejection standards, in the same sentence.

"…except in the case of qualitative disconformity, which shall be determined at the Buyer's reasonable and sole discretion. A certified, independent lab, agreed upon by all parties, will conduct both sampling and testing for said quantitative analysis."Inspection & testing

Quantitative questions — potency, contaminants — go to an independent lab both sides agree on. Qualitative questions go to the buyer alone. The contract's own quality criteria include "Pleasant aesthetic appearance and olfactory perception ('A' buds)" and "Machine trimmed or better", alongside a measurable one, "CBD content ≥12% on a dry weight basis".

So appearance and smell — judgements — are decided by the party paying, while potency is decided by a neutral lab. "Reasonable" limits that discretion in principle; what enforcing it would cost is explanation 3.

Redacted here. A {**REDACTED**} block sits immediately before the remedy that follows a failed inspection. What that clause says is not in the filed document, and this page will not guess at it.
The standard this is measured byA "sole discretion" term places the decision with the named party. A "reasonable" qualifier makes that decision reviewable — by whoever the contract sends disputes to.
3 · what it costs to disagree

Multnomah County, Oregon — and each side pays its own lawyers.

"Jurisdiction and venue shall be proper in Multnomah County, Oregon." … "Any dispute or claim … will be resolved by binding arbitration, in Multnomah County, Oregon rather than in court" … "each party shall be responsible for their own attorney fees. Including any and all appeals or petitions therefrom."Governing Law · Arbitration

Every dispute is heard in one county in Oregon, by binding arbitration, and each side funds its own representation regardless of who wins. For anyone not near Portland, the cost of pursuing a claim is travel plus counsel plus arbitrator, and it is not recoverable.

This is what gives explanation 2 its force. A rejection decided at the buyer's discretion is reviewable in principle — in a forum whose cost may exceed the value of the load being disputed.

The standard this is measured byA fee-shifting clause ("the losing party pays") makes small claims worth pursuing. Its absence — "each party bears its own" — makes the cost of enforcement a fixed toll on the party who has to travel.
4 · how long is this agreement

The document gives four different answers.

"a single occurrence, one month, integrated written contract"Entire Agreement
delivery schedule across June, July, August3 months
"FOB … during the initial three month engagement"3 months
"for the remainder of the term of the agreement"undefined

These are four statements about the same agreement's duration, in four sections. The "entire agreement" clause — the paragraph most readers skip because it usually says nothing — is the one that says one month.

The FOB term matters on its own: "All Product is FOB (Free On Board) from Supplier's location during the initial three month engagement", meaning the supplier's freight responsibility ends at their own gate for that period. What governs afterwards is not stated in the visible text.

The standard this is measured byAn integration clause defines what the agreement consists of and how long it runs. Where other provisions state different durations, which controls is a question of construction — resolved by the forum named in explanation 3.
5 · a term that works in the seller's favour

The inspection window is bounded at three days.

"Unless otherwise agreed upon in writing by each of the parties, Buyer shall have a 3-day inspection period commencing upon delivery to inspect the Seller's product"Inspection Period

This one is worth noticing because it limits the buyer. Inspection runs for three days from delivery, not indefinitely — so a load cannot be rejected weeks later on second thoughts.

What the document does not say is what happens when those three days pass. There is no clause stating that product is deemed accepted, and none stating that the right to reject lapses. The window has a start and a length; the visible text does not state its effect.

The standard this is measured byMany supply agreements pair an inspection period with an express acceptance term (a term to the effect that failure to reject within the period constitutes acceptance). This document contains the period without the paired term.
6 · what happens when someone fails

Every listed remedy runs one direction.

"should product fail to meet the contract requirements, Seller will remedy failure through any and/or all the following methods: · Price Reduction… · Breach Cure Periods: Seller shall have 30 days in which to cure any breach… · Accommodation Discount: This discount is for any delay or lack of continuity due solely to Seller's actions."Remedies

Three remedies are enumerated and all three are things the seller provides — a price reduction, a cure period the seller uses, a discount for delays the seller caused. The document does not enumerate what follows if the buyer does not order, does not pay, or does not take delivery.

Read alongside explanation 1, that is the shape of the whole agreement: the buyer holds an option on 240,000 lb, and no consequence is written down for declining to exercise it.

The standard this is measured byRemedy clauses are ordinarily reciprocal — each party's failures and the other's recourse. Where only one party's failures are enumerated, the other's are left to default law and to the forum in explanation 3.
